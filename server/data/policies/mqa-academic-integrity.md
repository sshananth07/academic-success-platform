# Malaysian Qualifications Agency (MQA) — Academic Integrity Guidelines for Malaysian Higher Education Institutions
**Code of Practice for Programme Accreditation (COPPA) — Academic Integrity Supplement**
**Edition 2.0 | 2024**

---

## 1. Preamble

The Malaysian Qualifications Agency (MQA) is mandated under the Malaysian Qualifications Agency Act 2007 to establish and maintain standards for higher education in Malaysia. Academic integrity (integriti akademik) is a foundational requirement for accreditation under the Malaysian Qualifications Framework (MQF). This document supplements the MQA Code of Practice for Programme Accreditation (COPPA 3.0) with specific guidance on academic misconduct, salah laku akademik, and institutional obligations in the context of emerging AI technologies.

---

## 2. Definition of Academic Misconduct (Salah Laku Akademik)

MQA defines academic misconduct as any action by a student, academic staff member, or institutional actor that compromises the authenticity, originality, fairness, or integrity of academic work or assessment processes. The following seven categories constitute academic misconduct under MQA guidelines:

### 2.1 Plagiarism

Presenting another person's — or an AI system's — words, ideas, data, or creative works as one's own without adequate attribution. Plagiarism encompasses:

- **Verbatim copying:** Reproducing text word-for-word from a source without quotation marks and proper citation, regardless of source (print, digital, AI-generated).
- **Mosaic plagiarism (patchwriting):** Replacing individual words or phrases in copied text while preserving the original sentence structure without attribution.
- **Paraphrase plagiarism:** Restating another's ideas in different words without citation.
- **Self-plagiarism:** Resubmitting one's own previously graded work in a different course or assessment without disclosure and instructor permission.
- **Translated plagiarism:** Translating a source from another language and presenting it as original work without attribution.

### 2.2 Contract Cheating (Penipuan Kontrak)

Outsourcing academic work to another person — human or AI system — and submitting the completed work as one's own. This includes engaging ghost-writing services, online essay mills, or using AI tools to generate complete assignments.

### 2.3 AI-Assisted Misconduct

Using artificial intelligence tools to produce academic work submitted as the student's original intellectual contribution, where such use has not been disclosed and is not permitted by the assessment rules. MQA notes that AI-assisted misconduct is functionally equivalent to contract cheating and is treated accordingly.

### 2.4 Fabrication and Falsification

Manufacturing data, results, citations, or any factual content that was not obtained through legitimate means. This includes using AI tools to generate fictitious research data, fabricated interview responses, or non-existent references.

### 2.5 Collusion

Unauthorised collaboration between students on work that is required to be completed independently. This includes sharing AI prompts, AI-generated outputs, or completed assignments between students for submission as individual work.

### 2.6 Impersonation

Having another person — human or AI — represent oneself in any academic assessment, whether examination, oral presentation, interview, laboratory session, or online assessment.

### 2.7 Facilitating Misconduct

Providing assistance to another student that enables them to commit academic misconduct. This includes sharing completed assignments, selling academic work, or providing AI-generated content to peers for submission.

## 3. Plagiarism Taxonomy and Severity Classification

MQA uses a severity-based classification to guide institutional decision-making:

| Severity | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Level 1 (Minor) | Unintentional, limited in scope | Improper citation format, missing quotation marks for short passage |
| Level 2 (Moderate) | Intentional or negligent, limited scope | Paraphrase plagiarism of one source, undisclosed minor AI assistance |
| Level 3 (Serious) | Intentional, significant scope | Plagiarism of substantial portion, contract cheating for minor assignment |
| Level 4 (Severe) | Intentional, systemic, or repeat | Wholly plagiarised or AI-generated major submission, repeat offending |

## 4. Institutional Obligations Under MQA Code of Practice

Higher education institutions (HEIs) seeking or maintaining MQA accreditation must:

4.1 **Publish a current academic integrity policy** that is accessible to all students and staff, reviewed at minimum every three years, and aligned with MQA guidelines.

4.2 **Establish a faculty-level Academic Integrity Committee** with authority to investigate allegations and recommend penalties.

4.3 **Establish a university-level Senate Academic Integrity Board** to hear appeals and adjudicate Tier 3/4 matters.

4.4 **Provide academic integrity training** to all new students during orientation, with annual refresher materials available digitally.

4.5 **Use plagiarism detection software** for all written assessments exceeding 500 words. The use of detection software does not replace human judgment in determining misconduct.

4.6 **Maintain comprehensive records** of academic misconduct investigations, findings, and outcomes for a minimum of seven years. Records must be available to MQA auditors upon request.

4.7 **Report statistical summaries** of academic misconduct cases annually to MQA as part of the Quality Assurance Review (QAR) process.

---

## 5. Audit Requirements

MQA conducts periodic programme audits to assess institutional compliance with academic integrity standards. Audit reviewers will examine:

- Policy currency and accessibility
- Student and staff awareness (surveyed during audit visits)
- Case investigation records and consistency of outcomes
- Detection tool usage logs
- Curriculum design for originality promotion
- Assessment design to mitigate misconduct opportunities

Failure to demonstrate adequate academic integrity systems is grounds for conditions, deferral, or withdrawal of programme accreditation.

---

## 6. Whistleblower Protections

MQA guidelines require HEIs to establish confidential reporting mechanisms for students and staff to report suspected academic misconduct without fear of retaliation. Whistleblower protections must include:

- Anonymous reporting channels (digital or physical)
- Explicit anti-retaliation policies with penalties for reprisal
- Protection from grade penalties or other academic consequences for good-faith reporters
- Clear timelines for investigation response to reporters

---

## 7. Appeals Process

Students found guilty of academic misconduct have the right to a fair appeals process. MQA requires HEIs to ensure:

7.1 Written notification of findings and proposed penalties within 14 calendar days of investigation conclusion.

7.2 Right to appeal within 14 calendar days of receiving the written decision.

7.3 An independent appeals panel not including original investigators.

7.4 Appeals resolved within 21 calendar days of submission.

7.5 Final appeal to the university Senate Academic Integrity Board, with written reasons provided regardless of outcome.

---

## 8. Cross-Institutional Recognition of Sanctions

Sanctions for serious academic misconduct (Level 3–4 under Section 3) are reportable to MQA and may be shared with other Malaysian public universities through the MQA Academic Integrity Registry. A student suspended or expelled from one public university for academic misconduct may be refused admission to other public universities for the duration of the sanction.

---

## 9. Record-Keeping Requirements

Institutions must maintain the following records for each academic misconduct case:

- Original allegation documentation
- Investigation timeline and evidence collected
- Student's written response or denial
- Committee deliberation notes
- Final decision and penalty imposed
- Appeals record (if any)
- Implementation of penalty confirmation

All records must be stored securely in the student's academic file. Records are subject to Malaysia's Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA) and must not be disclosed to third parties without lawful basis.
