# APA 7th Edition Citation Guide for AI-Assisted Academic Work
## Universiti Awam Malaysia Library Services — Academic Writing Support
**Updated: January 2025 | Aligned with APA Publication Manual, 7th Edition**

## 1. Introduction to APA 7th Edition

The American Psychological Association (APA) 7th Edition citation style is the standard referencing format at Universiti Awam Malaysia for most academic disciplines, including social sciences, education, psychology, business, health sciences, and interdisciplinary programmes. This guide covers the core citation formats and provides specific guidance on citing AI-generated content — a requirement that became necessary as large language model tools became widely used in academic work from 2023 onwards.

**Why proper citation matters:** Citations serve three core functions: (1) they give credit to original authors for their intellectual contributions, (2) they allow readers to locate and verify sources, and (3) they demonstrate the breadth and rigour of a writer's engagement with the scholarly literature. Incorrect citations — including fabricated or AI-generated citations — undermine all three functions and constitute academic misconduct under UAM policy.

## 2. Standard APA In-Text Citation Format

### 2.1 Basic In-Text Citations

**Author–date format:** Place the author's last name and the year of publication in parentheses following a quote or paraphrase.

Single author:
> The relationship between digital literacy and academic performance is well-established (Ahmad, 2021).

Two authors (always use ampersand &):
> Students benefit from structured feedback on their writing (Lee & Tan, 2023).

Three or more authors (use "et al." from first citation):
> Research on mobile learning in Malaysia shows consistent engagement gains (Rashid et al., 2022).

**Quoting directly** (include page number or paragraph):
> "Academic integrity forms the bedrock of all scholarly endeavour" (Mohd Noor, 2020, p. 47).

**No author:** Use the title (italicised for standalone works, in quotation marks for articles), followed by year.
> ("Understanding Plagiarism," 2019)

**No date:** Use "n.d." (no date).
> (Universiti Awam Malaysia, n.d.)

### 2.2 In-Text Citations for Multiple Sources

Separate multiple citations with semicolons, ordered alphabetically:
> Several studies confirm this relationship (Ibrahim, 2022; Santos et al., 2021; Yusoff, 2023).

## 3. Reference List Format

The reference list appears at the end of the paper, titled **References** (centred, bold). Entries are listed alphabetically by first author's surname. Use a hanging indent (first line flush, subsequent lines indented 1.27 cm / 0.5 inches).

### 3.1 Journal Articles

**Basic format:**
> Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article in sentence case. *Title of Periodical in Title Case and Italics*, *Volume*(Issue), Page–Page. https://doi.org/xxxxx

**Example:**
> Azman, H., & Nor, F. M. (2022). Digital academic writing challenges among Malaysian undergraduates. *Asian Journal of University Education*, *18*(3), 112–128. https://doi.org/10.24191/ajue.v18i3.18543

**Without DOI (include URL if available):**
> Lim, K. Y., & Razak, A. (2021). AI literacy in Malaysian higher education. *Journal of Educational Technology*, *15*(2), 88–104. https://www.example-journal.edu.my/volume15

### 3.2 Books

> Author, A. A. (Year). *Title of work: Capital letter also for subtitle*. Publisher. https://doi.org/xxxxx

**Example:**
> Abdullah, Z. (2019). *Academic writing for Malaysian university students* (2nd ed.). Dewan Bahasa dan Pustaka.

### 3.3 Book Chapters (Edited Books)

> Author, A. A. (Year). Title of chapter. In E. E. Editor & F. F. Editor (Eds.), *Title of book* (pp. xx–xx). Publisher.

### 3.4 Websites and Online Documents

> Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). *Title of page in sentence case*. Site Name. URL

**Example:**
> Ministry of Higher Education Malaysia. (2023, April 15). *Framework for academic integrity in public universities*. MOHE. https://www.mohe.gov.my/integrity-framework

**No author or date:**
> Universiti Awam Malaysia. (n.d.). *Student academic integrity policy*. Retrieved January 10, 2025, from https://www.uam.edu.my/integrity

## 4. Citing AI-Generated Content

The APA 7th Edition (updated guidance, 2023) establishes specific formats for citing AI-generated text. The core principle is: **if you use it, you must cite it.** AI tools cannot be listed as authors because they are not legally accountable for their outputs. Instead, the company that develops the AI tool is treated as the author.

### 4.1 How to Cite AI-Generated Text

**Use this format:**

> Developer/Company. (Year). *AI Tool Name* (Model version or description) [Large language model]. URL

**Examples:**

Citing ChatGPT (OpenAI):
> OpenAI. (2024). *ChatGPT* (GPT-4 version, January 2024) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com

Citing Google Gemini:
> Google. (2024). *Gemini* (Gemini 1.5 Pro version) [Large language model]. https://gemini.google.com

Citing Claude (Anthropic):
> Anthropic. (2024). *Claude* (Claude 3.5 Sonnet version) [Large language model]. https://claude.ai

Citing Microsoft Copilot:
> Microsoft. (2024). *Microsoft Copilot* [Large language model]. https://copilot.microsoft.com

**In-text citation for AI-generated content:**
> When asked to explain the concept of plagiarism, the AI tool provided the following definition: "Plagiarism involves presenting another's ideas or words as your own without attribution" (OpenAI, 2024).

### 4.2 How to Cite AI-Assisted Paraphrasing

When you use an AI tool to help rephrase your own writing (with disclosure), the in-text citation is not required for individual sentences. However, you must include the general disclosure statement in your AI Use Declaration appendix (see UAM AI-Use Policy, Section 5). If you incorporate AI paraphrased content that draws on another source, cite the original source, not the AI tool.

### 4.3 Citing AI-Generated Images and Figures

> Developer/Company. (Year). *Description of the image in sentence case* [AI-generated image]. AI Tool Name. URL if available.

**Example:**
> Google. (2024). *Diagram illustrating the relationship between machine learning and natural language processing* [AI-generated image]. Gemini. https://gemini.google.com

**Caption format (in paper):** Figure 1. *Conceptual diagram of machine learning components* (Google, 2024).

## 5. When AI Use Does NOT Require a Citation

Not all AI tool use requires a formal citation. The following uses are generally considered equivalent to spell-check or grammar tools and do not require citation, **unless your course policy specifies otherwise**:

5.1 **Spell-check and autocorrect** — standard built-in spell-check in word processors (Microsoft Word, Google Docs).

5.2 **Basic grammar correction** — correcting grammatical errors flagged by tools like Grammarly Standard (free tier), where the substantive content is entirely the student's own.

5.3 **Translation for comprehension** — using Google Translate to understand a source in a foreign language, where the student then writes their own analysis.

5.4 **Search query suggestions** — using AI to suggest search terms for academic databases, where the student independently searches and evaluates all sources.

**Important:** Even when citation is not required, **disclosure** of AI use may still be required under UAM AI-Use Policy if the use was substantive. When in doubt, disclose. Disclosure is always permissible; concealment is never permissible.

## 6. Self-Citation Rules

Students may cite their own previously published work following standard APA format. However:

6.1 **Self-plagiarism is prohibited** — submitting the same or substantially similar work for multiple assessments without instructor permission and disclosure violates UAM and MQA policies.

6.2 When building on your own prior work (e.g., expanding a conference paper into a thesis chapter), cite the prior work clearly and disclose to the supervisor that the current work incorporates or extends prior submitted work.

**Format for self-citation:**
> Rajan, S. (2023). Barriers to technology adoption in rural Malaysian schools. *Malaysian Journal of Education*, *48*(1), 55–72. https://doi.org/10.example

## 7. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | Example of Error | Correct Approach |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Fabricating a citation | Citing a paper that doesn't exist, or including a DOI that leads nowhere | Only cite sources you have actually accessed |
| Using AI-generated citations | Copying reference lists generated by ChatGPT without verification | Always verify that every cited source exists by checking the actual publication |
| Missing "et al." | (Ahmad, Ibrahim, Rashid, Yusoff, 2019) | (Ahmad et al., 2019) for 3+ authors |
| Incorrect DOI format | doi: 10.1234/example | https://doi.org/10.1234/example |
| Outdated edition | Following APA 6th edition rules | Use APA 7th edition (2020 onwards) — note changes to running head, DOI format, author lists |
| Listing AI as author | "ChatGPT. (2024). Essay on…" | OpenAI. (2024). *ChatGPT*… |
| Missing retrieval date for stable content | Always adding "Retrieved on…" | Only add retrieval date for content likely to change (e.g., wiki pages, databases). Omit for journal articles and books. |

## 8. DOI Formatting

Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs) provide permanent links to published academic content. APA 7th Edition requires DOIs to be presented as hyperlinks:

- **Correct:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0000165-000
- **Incorrect:** DOI: 10.1037/0000165-000 or doi:10.1037/0000165-000

Not all articles have DOIs. For articles without DOIs, include a stable URL if available, or omit the URL for print-only sources.
